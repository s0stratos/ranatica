# INTRODUCTION TO LATIN GRAMMAR

This is a really short introduction to latin grammar.

## THE VERB

Verbs in latin a primarily characterized by having a **mood**.

They are also typically inflected for **tense**.

### MOODS

Latin verbs have 3 finite moods :
- INDICATIVE
- SUBJUNCTIVE
- IMPERATIVE

And 1 non-finite mood :
- INFINITIVE 

Latin also has participles :
- PRESENT (NT) - ACTIVE
- FUTURE (Ã™R) - ACTIVE
- PAST (TU) - VARIABLE ([see alignment section](#ALIGNMENT))
- ND PARTICIPLE (ND) - PASSIVE

Finally, latin also has different verbal nouns, whose forms are reminiscent of some participles.
- SUPINE (TU)
- GERUNDIVE (ND)
- IO FORMS _(EARLY LATIN)_

Finite forms are inflected for **person**.

Participles are inflected for **subject agreement**

| TYPE         | MORPHOLOGY                                                           |
|--------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| FINITE FORMS | MOOD, TENSE, PERSON, (ALIGNMENT [see alignment section](#ALIGNMENT)) |
| INFINITIVE   | TENSE, (ALIGNMENT [see alignment section](#ALIGNMENT))               |
| VERBAL NOUN  | CASE                                                                 |
| PARTICIPLE   | TENSE, SUBJECT AGREEMENT                                             |  

### ALIGNMENT

If you observe latin verbs in general, you'll see both "active"-forms and a "r"-forms exist.

This is a property of the verbs to either have both types of forms or only "r"-forms.

Verbs that only have "r"-forms are called deponent verbs.

"Deponent" just means that those verbs give up on having "active" forms.

The patterns are the following:

|                        | ACTIVE VERBS WITH A DIRECT OBJECT                                                                 | DEPONENT VERBS WITH A DIRECT OBJECT                                                                              |
|------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| FINITE FORMS - ACTIVE  | X[nominative] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>panem edo                                     | DOESNT EXIST                                                                                                     |
| FINITE FORMS - R-FORMS | Y[nominative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>panis estur                                                 | X[nominative] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>montes molior                                                | 
| INFINITIVE - ACTIVE    | X[-] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>uideo [eum panem edere]                                | DOESNT EXIST                                                                                                     |
| INFINITIVES - R-FORMS  | Y[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>uideo [panem edi]                                                    | X[-] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>uideo [eum montes moliri]                                             |
| VERBAL NOUNS           | X[-] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>abeo [panem esum]<br/>fessus sum [panem edendo]        | X[-] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>abeo [montes molitum]<br/>fessus sum montes moliendo                  |
| (ACTIVE) PARTICIPLES   | X[-] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>[panem edens] gaudeo<br/>panem esurus sum              | X[-] Y[accusative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>montes moliens gaudeo<br/>montes moliturus sum<br/>montes molitus sum |
| (PASSIVE) PARTICIPLES  | Y[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>panis edendus est<br/>panis iam esus est</br>tempus est panis edendi | Y[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)<br/>montes moliendos sunt</br>tempus est montes moliendi                                | 

|                        | ACTIVE VERBS WITHOUT A DIRECT OBJECT | DEPONENT VERBS WITHOUT A DIRECT OBJECT |
|------------------------|--------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| FINITE FORMS - ACTIVE  | X[nominative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)    | DOESNT EXIST                           |
| FINITE FORMS - R-FORMS | (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)                  | X[nominative] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)      |
| INFINITIVE - ACTIVE    | X[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)             | DOESNT EXIST                           |
| INFINITIVE - R-FORMS   | (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)                  | X[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)               |
| VERBAL NOUN            | X[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)             | X[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)               |
| PARTICIPLE             | X[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)             | X[-] (OTHER COMPLEMENTS)               |

The past participle in -tu/-su in the most loosely correlative with the rest of the verb behavior

The general case is that **being part of the same paradigms as finite forms they follow the same behavior as the finite r-forms**.
But there are actually **lexical exceptions** to this.
